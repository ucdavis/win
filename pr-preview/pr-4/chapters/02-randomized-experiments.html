1 Introduction to Randomized Experiments

Randomized experiments are considered the gold standard for causal inference. In a randomized experiment, treatment assignment is determined by a random mechanism, which ensures that treatment groups are comparable in expectation with respect to all baseline characteristics, both measured and unmeasured.

2 Why Randomize?

Randomization provides several key benefits:

  1. Eliminates confounding: By randomly assigning treatment, we break any association between treatment and pre-treatment covariates
  2. Balances groups: Treated and untreated groups are comparable in expectation on all baseline characteristics
  3. Simplifies analysis: The average treatment effect can be estimated by a simple difference in means
  4. Provides validity: Results have a clear causal interpretation without requiring untestable assumptions

3 Mathematical Framework

In a randomized experiment with treatment \(A \in \{0,1\}\) and outcome \(Y\), we have:

\[P(A=a|L) = P(A=a)\]

where \(L\) represents all pre-treatment covariates. This means treatment assignment is independent of all baseline characteristics. As a result, the average causal effect can be estimated as:

\[E[Y^{a=1}] - E[Y^{a=0}] = E[Y|A=1] - E[Y|A=0]\]

The right-hand side is simply the difference in observed mean outcomes between treatment groups, which can be estimated from the data without bias.

4 Types of Randomization

There are several approaches to randomization:

Simple Randomization: Each individual is assigned to treatment or control with a fixed probability (e.g., 50%). This is easy to implement but can result in imbalanced group sizes in small samples.

Stratified Randomization: Randomization is performed separately within strata defined by important covariates. This ensures balance on these key variables and can improve precision.

Block Randomization: Randomization is done in blocks to ensure approximately equal numbers in each treatment group. This is particularly useful in sequential trials.

Cluster Randomization: Groups (clusters) of individuals are randomized together. This is used when individual randomization is not feasible.

5 Analysis of Randomized Trials

The analysis of randomized experiments is straightforward due to the exchangeability guaranteed by randomization:

  1. Intention-to-treat (ITT): Compare groups as randomized, regardless of actual treatment received. This preserves randomization and provides a valid causal estimate.

  2. Per-protocol: Compare only those who adhered to their assigned treatment. This can be biased if adherence is related to potential outcomes.

  3. As-treated: Analyze based on treatment actually received. This is essentially an observational analysis and loses the benefits of randomization.

The ITT analysis is generally preferred as it maintains the validity of randomization and provides an unbiased estimate of the causal effect of treatment assignment.

6 Limitations

Despite their advantages, randomized experiments have limitations:

  • Ethical constraints: Not all questions can be studied via randomization
  • Cost: Randomized trials can be expensive and time-consuming
  • External validity: Results may not generalize to other populations
  • Compliance: Non-adherence can complicate analysis
  • Feasibility: Some interventions cannot be randomized

These limitations explain why observational studies remain important and why methods for causal inference from observational data are necessary.

7 Summary

Randomized experiments provide the strongest foundation for causal inference by ensuring exchangeability through random treatment assignment. The causal effect can be estimated by a simple comparison of outcomes between randomized groups. However, practical and ethical constraints limit the applicability of randomization, necessitating methods for causal inference from observational data, which we will explore in subsequent chapters.
